Simon,

I hope you don't mind me sharing this somewhat serious account on my personal website. FYI - this page is unlisted, so only visible via the direct link.

My hope is that this story might help you to better understand the "why" behind what I'm doing with Impulse, and as a result, that you may have renewed enthusiasm for joining as an advisor to help shape the company's growth and impact.

# The Descent

In 2017, I was in Melbourne, Australia, and outwardly, life looked good — I had a loving relationship, a nice apartment, and a rewarding job as a team lead at a large ecommerce company.

But then, a new habit started to form. After work, I'd drop onto the couch and spend hours playing online chess and watching YouTube videos. While I originally saw these activities as a way to unwind or learn, I soon found myself stuck late into the night, often without eating.

My chess playing switched from 10 minute games to 3 minute, and then even 1 minute games - each being a flurry of activity and a corresponding burst of neurochemicals. At the same time, YouTube’s algorithm started taking me into weird, dopamine-inducing territory, including police chases and [plane crashes](https://www.youtube.com/@theflightchannel). I suppose the algorithm was all-to-happy to guide me there with the payoff being more clicks and ads!

# The Crisis

One night, I found myself playing chess till the early morning, and thinking:

“I could stop, right?”… [NEW GAME]<br />
“But If I really wanted to stop, I could…Right?” … [NEW GAME]<br />
"Ok, so I want to stop. Now what?”… [NEW GAME]

I eventually mustered up the willpower to throw my phone to the other side of the couch, and went to the bed - a sorry sight! ...only to retrieve it moments later and resume playing.

It was clear I had a genuine addiction, even if others often didn't view it seriously. I also sometimes felt a little guilty when I might compare it to other "more serious" issues that others were facing, and I remain very thankful that it never escalated to something more harmful, like substances or gambling. Still, I felt I was getting a vidid sense of what it can feel like to be truly trapped in a loop, and to not see the way out.

(One small note: the funny thing about this experience? My chess rating was consistently getting worse over this time. So much for "enriching" my brain!)

# The search for change and my "Impulse" realization

One evening, while stuck in this state, I had the original idea for impulse: a wearable pendant that you could press when you first felt the “impulse moment” (the craving or urge to engage in the unwanted pattern of thinking or behavior). I imagined it physically vibrating after a press, then buzzing again after a 2 minute delay. An accompanying app could prompt you after the first press to complete a breathing exercise, to walk, or take some other action to guide you through the moment. Importantly, there'd also be a "debriefing" component, allowing the app to adapt and learn with you.

Hardware was hard, and progress was slow!

Personally speaking, I had the idea to change cities as "change of scenery", and moved to Berlin, hoping a fresh start would break the cycle. It worked initially—but soon, old habits resurfaced.

While in Berlin, I also witnessed others battling more severe addictions, and my then girl friend’s dad, witnessed the dad of someone close to me succumb tragically to alcoholism. I never want to use stories like these for “emotional value”, because for themost part, they honestly weren't “my trauma”, but it became a part of the fabric of my experience in Berlin, and it experiences like these - along with my own struggles - that motivated me to press on with Impulse.

# A Glimpse of What a Clear Mind Can Feel Like

A close friend in Berlin was following my story and my unhelpful pattern of behavior, and tried to help one day. She insited that I give her my phone so she could lock it away for a full week. Once I relented, I walked back into my room, sat on the bed, and had a thought: “now what?”

I literally didn’t know what to do, as for so long, almost every spare moment was filled with some dopaminergic, digital distraction that kept my brain locked in this very unnatural state.

After a while, I remember grabbing my laptop and responding to a long-neglected email from my grandma, making some food, and cleaning. I returned to my room, and again thought, “Now what?”. I then first began to enjoy the feeling of just [doing nothing](https://blog.impulse.training/untangle-time-a-different-kind-of-mindfulness-a0a6a7fd8861), and I think my mind quickly started moving towards a state of better balance.

After about a week, this new balance manifested in my body - I felt my arms, legs, and the rest of my body in a more vivid way, I started sleeping better and even remembering my dreams (which I hadn't for years), and even felt a new sense of joy and wellbeing while doing something as simple as walking down the street.

# Clarifying Impulse's Mission

However, after some time, I got my phone back, and - as you might have guessed - I pretty quickly slipped back into the same patterns (though not always to the same degree of intensity or severity).

This lead to a new realization: Impulse needed to give people tools to overcome their impulses, not by modifying their environment to remove the source of triggers, but by giving them tools to consciously “ride-out” the impulse moment towards a values-aligned choice.

After a 2 year interlude as a tech lead for another startup that took me to San Franciso, I took the plunge and went full-time on Impulse, securing some initial funding.

I could go into more detail about those early days, and how ironically, it was partially an inappropriate focus on “quality” that slowed progress.

But mor importantly, I honestly never fully broke free of my impulses during this phase. That is, until 3 weeks ago!

# Personal Breakthrough

It’s been 3 weeks and 2 days now that I’ve had a 100% win rate with my key impulses that I’d been tracking - with Twitter being the most recent primary one.

I’m not sure if Andrew was surprised that when he first started engaging with me with some accountability, that my “win streak” was non-existent, and I was still having “setbacks”. While it occurred to me that this reality might have undermined my “authority” to build this product, it also occurred to me that the opposite could also be true.

It has been more than three weeks now that I’ve been living in a state of very pleasant clarity and perhaps "flow", with many of the same benefits that I mentioned earlier, including remembering some dreams again (though my sleep habits happen to be affected by some current medication that I'm making a change on).

# What's Next

Now, I want to codify what has best helped me overcome my impulses into the impulse app, and provide a way for others - both experts and members of the community - to contribute their own "gameplans" and encouragement that can become immediately available to people during their "impulse moments".

We have a long way to go, but with several key opportunities and milestones coming up, there's no time to waste to bring Impulse to the world to help people to overcome their impulses.
