Simon!

I hope you don't mind me sharing this story on my rather whimsical website. I made it many years ago and don't often update it, but I hope the format isn't too tiring for the eyes to read.

FYI - this page is unlisted, so only visible via the direct link.

To share this story, I want to re-wind to 2017, when I was living and working in Melbourne, Australia. From the outside, I was doing well - I had a girlfriend, friends, hobbies, a nice apartment. I had a great job as a team lead of a successful ecommece company, so things seemed pretty good - and they were pretty good!

But increasingly, each night after work, I’d drop onto the couch and start playing online chess on my phone. I started with 10 minute chess games, and I’d analyze each game afterwards to identify mistakes and missed opportunities. My ELO (rating) steadily increased from week to week, and while I never reached a particularly impressive rating, it was a sign that I was mentally engaged, and learning. I’d also hop between chess and YouTube videos, initially a lot of physics, philosophy, and other “wholesome” video content.

“It’s enriching!” - I’d say to myself.

However, after a while, switched to 3 minute chess games, as I found the rapid-fire moves and constant surprises very stimulating - and this was increasingly what my body and brain craved. I also switched to more stimulating, shocking, or extreme YouTube videos, and the algorithm moved with me, guiding into dark and strange places while high on a cocktail of adrenaline, cortisol, dopamine, and serotonin.

For example, I watched almost every “police chase” video I could find, with long, drawn-out scenes in which a squadron of cars would chase a suspect, often resulting a crash that was occasionally devastating. Police shootings, also. I also watched every single video I could from TheFlightChannel, some of them, some of them several times over, as they followed a similar template - a long and drawn-out build up escalating into an increasingly nerve-wracking conclustion, with a fiery ending. It wasn’t that I had a fundamental morbidity, it’s just that these particular videos were somehow delivered more to my brain of what it was craving, and all-knowing algorithm was all-to-happy to take me there.

And before you know it - I was hooked. I told you a recent call that I found myself stuck one night playing chess till the early hours of the morning, and saying to myself:

“Yeah, but I could stop, right?”…. [NEW GAME]
“If I really wanted to stop, I could…” “…Right?” … [NEW GAME]
“Well... do I want to stop? Yes. I want to stop.”… ... [NEW GAME]

So, it became clear that I had a serious addiction, although I was grateful that I never had any temptation to “switch lanes” into something more harmful (e.g., a substance, gambling, or something that was directly harmful to others).

But despite the apparent “harmlessness” of my situation to others (people would rarely take it seriously when I told them), I felt I could understand Einstein’s quote: “Chess holds its master in its own bonds, shackling the mind and brain so that the inner freedom of the very strongest must suffer”. Did you know that he (Einstein) consciously chose to avoid chess for most of his productive life, even though he initially enjoyed it and was gifted at it - as he reali`ed that it could potentially consume his entire life? I think I understand this - in my mind, chess represents "an Infinity", and the human brain can get lost in such “infinities” (YouTube is also practically "an Infinity”, as one could never watch all of the videos that have been uploaded, as more are constantly going up)

I remember tears falling down my face that night, at around 1 or 2am, as I continued playing chess despite a clear desire stemming from my true core to stop - but a simple inability to do so. My eyes kept closing, to the point where I could barely see the screen, but still, I tried to play new games.

I eventually mustered up the willpower to throw my phone to the other side of the couch, went to the bed - a fairly sorry sight! Then, after a few minutes of feeling sorry for myself and feeling physical twitches and shakes, I got up, got my phone, brought it back to my bed with me, and tapped that button on screen: [NEW GAME]!

One small note: the funny thing about this experience? My ELO rating was consistently getting worse, every single week, across all game formats (1 minute, 3 minute, 10 minute). It wasn’t even me playing the game any more. I felt it was the game playing itself. I was HOOKED.

This continued for 1-2 years, during which time, I was increasingly scattered, distracted, and ineffective in the things I would try to apply myself to. I would wake up, and play chess. I would play chess or watch videos on the tram all the way into to work, and right up until I sat down at my work chair. I would close my eyes at any hour of the day, and all I would see is chess moves (and not even “good” or “inspired” ones!)

I maintained decent performance with my job through this time, despite this, and management asked whether I’d consider moving towards a more senior leadership track. It’s funny how that particular conversation - and the idea of a career in management of a bland and uninspiring ecommerce company - triggered an instant sense that I needed to quit my job and get onto my true path.

Anyway, after about 2 years of this being my reality, I had the original idea for impulse - a wearable pendant that you could press when you first felt the “impulse moment” (the craving, or urge to engage in the unwanted pattern of thinking or behavior). I imagined it physically vibrating in response to the press, at which point, I’d take two minutes to walk, distract myself, or pause, until the second buzz two minutes later. That second buzz would be a prompt for reflection: “Do I still want to do the thing?” By that point, you might be in a different physical location; you might have completed a breathing exercise or something similar; or the urge may have simply abated.

However, this “hardware pendant” presented some difficulties. I began looking at off-the-shelf hardware options, but my ability to invest in the project was limited, as I was working full-time.

Instead, I had the idea to change cities as a way to “shake up” my situation enough to hopefully break the cycle, which at this point was all-consuming. I moved to Berlin, which was a whirlwind in many respects, and was actually largely free of this unwanted pattern of behavior for around 2 months.

And then, before you know it, I slipped right back into the loop. I maintained this “functioning addict” mode as I moved to a new flatshare, this time in a “party district” of Berlin. I lived with 8 others in a rooftop in this building: https://maps.app.goo.gl/b44chHSSNi1kKNiH6

While there, I saw flatmates with various types and levels of addiction, including 2-bottles-of-wine-per-night-Anna, the many homeless, and Khaled from Syria, (who could never return, as he had left the compulsory military service without leave, a crime that is punishable by death), who paramedics had to resuscitate from ketamine overdoses in which he was blue and stiff. While I had my own experiences with substances, the only one I had any kind of real addiction to was nicotine, in the form of vaping or cigarettes… But I could definitely understand how and why people might reach for a substance to help process some negative emotion.

I also followed along with my then-girlfriend’s dad, who was battling alcoholism. When I met him, he was yellow, and wearing only underpants, but he hugged me, and later, Sashi told me that he knew what that moment was. He died 2 months later.

Now, I never want to use that story for “emotional value”, because it’s honestly not true that it was somehow “my trauma”. I simply witnessed another family go through that difficult experience, and was a support to my girlfriend.

But it was experiences like these - along with my own struggles - that filled me with an existential longing to do what I can to help humanity to break the cycle of our impulses.

In any case, a close friend in Berlin saw that I had essentially become “a wreck”, completely enchained to my chess and YouTube impulses. One day, she said to me: “Give me your phone!”, and despite my resistance, she insited, until I gave it to her, and she locked it away in her room, for a full week. I may have told you this already, but immediately after that I walked into my room, and sat on the bed, and had a thought: “now what?”. I literally didn’t know what to do, as it had been for so long that literally every spare moment of every day was filled with some dopaminergic, digital distraction that kept my brain locked in to this “escape” mode.

After a while, I remember grabbing my laptop and responding to an email to grandma that I’d left for 2 months (interestingly, the laptop was never really problematic in terms of impulses), and then getting up to make some food. After eating and cleaning up, I returned to my room, and sat on the bed, and thought, again, “Now what??”. I remember my hands and arms were still shaking, probably because of the extent that I’d pushed my brain neurochemically in terms of my motivation and reward cycle, but I quickly enjoyed the feeling of just doing nothing https://blog.impulse.training/untangle-time-a-different-kind-of-mindfulness-a0a6a7fd8861

After about a week, I started to feel physical changes. I felt my arms, legs, and the rest of my body in a new way, I started remembering my dreams and sleeping better, and I started to feel a new sense of joy and wellbeing while doing something as simple as walking down the street. It was amazing!

However, after some time, I got my phone back, and ultimately, I slipped back into the same patterns - though not always to the same degree of intensity or severity.

This lead to a clarification of the mission of impulse: to give people tools to overcome their impulses - not by modifying their environment to remove the source of triggers (e.g. safe houses for alcohol, “blocker” apps for phone addiction, etc), but by giving people tools to consciously “ride-out” the impulse moment. I imagined distractions, messages of encouragement from support people, challenges, breathing exercises or CBT techniques, and more, all available “in the impulse moment” with a single tap of a button. The vision was clear: impulse as an adaptive, just-in-time intervention that learns with the user and accompanies them on their their journey of overcoming.

In the meantime, I was working on my other main startup idea, ActionPact promo draft.mov, until a company called Motion came on the scene, solving my need perfectly (side note: I recommend them!). I can’t say for sure why I chose to work on ActionPact over Impulse. I think it was because I felt there was a clear market need and go to market strategy, but in hindsight, it feels like a distraction.

Anyway, I then got offered a job as a tech lead for an Australian start-up, and took it while still in Berlin, but eventually moved to San Francisco with the company. I learned a lot and really enjoyed supporting the team through 2 years of rapid growth, but eventually saw a that the founder and I were on divergent paths, and signalled my intention to transition. I gave notice that I wanted to go full-time on Impulse (with the view to make the pendant), although I had some credit card debt and little savings. The other senior engineer became Impulse’s first investor, which bought some time, and test hardware.

I could go into more detail about those early days, and how ironically, an excessive focus on “quality” actually held back the success of the company. That is, I wanted to maintain a high percentage of automated test coverage on the app, to ensure that the experience was bug-free for users.

But with learning curves for hardware, firmware, and native software, and with it just being myself on the project, and other expenses associated with our resident psychologist, I quickly burned through the $50k or so that I’d raised at the time, with little real traction to show for it.

(As an aside, this experience helped to inform my perspective on building and testing products)

There’s one more important part of this story, and that’s a confession that throughout this process of building impulse (or at least, trying to), I never fully broke free of my impulses - that is, until 3 weeks ago.

It’s been 3 weeks and 2 days now that I’ve had a 100% win rate with key impulses that I’ve been tracking - with Twitter being the primary impulse and the “evolution” of the original “black hole” problem (Which began as Chess, then shifted to primarily YouTube, then primarily Twitter).

I’m not sure if Andrew was surprised that when he first started engaging with me with daily accountability, my “win streak” was non-existent, and I was still having “setbacks” on many days.

I would have loved to have been surfing this epic win-streak at the time, but the reality was - I just wasn’t! I wasn’t doing too badly, all things considered, but my days could still be interspersed with episodes of zombie-like doom-scrolling.

While it occurred to me that this reality might have undermined my “authority” to build this product, it also occurred to me that the opposite is also true. As someone who constantly wrestled with this stuff and regularly had setbacks, surely I’m a qualified person to build a user interface that can act as a guide towards a values-aligned outcome!

One other comment - I think you know that I always did my best to track time diligently with projects such as Walk2Gether, where an accurate representation of hours was important, but the reality is that I wasn’t bringing my best to these projects - as my brain was still in a state of constant distraction.

Thankfully, as I said, it has been more than three weeks now that I’ve been living in a state of wonderful flow, with a recurrence of some of the same benefits that I mentioned earlier, including a heightended awareness of my physicality, new joy and lightness of being, and remembering dreams again (though my sleep habits are currently affected by some current medication).

If you've talked with people that are working to overcome some unwanted life pattern, you might have something similar, "This time, it's different! I swear!"

In my case, I do feel that it's a paradigm shift rather than a phase, but I don't ever want to "let my guard down" - as that has bitten me in the past.

Instead, I want to codefi what has best helped me overcome my impulses, and provide a way for others - both experts and community members - to contribute "gameplans" for overcoming that become immediately available to people that are struggling, in the moments that matter most.

If you'd like to continue on this journey with me, it would be an honor to have you as an advisor for Impulse.

Here's to overcoming!
Michael
